# Constructive Episodic Simulation in Dreams (2022)

Published in *PLOS One*, 2022. Extends Schacter & Addis's (2007) constructive episodic simulation hypothesis to dreaming.

## Key Finding

In a study of 469 dream reports from 48 college students:
- 53.5% of dreams traced to at least one specific past episodic memory
- 25.7% of dreams were linked to anticipated upcoming events (future-oriented)
- 43.9% incorporated fragments from multiple different sources
- 37.5% of future-oriented dreams also incorporated specific past memories

## What This Means

Dreams are not random — they recombine episodic memory fragments into novel scenarios. Crucially, 25.7% of dreams are explicitly prospective: they simulate anticipated future events, not just process the past. And the majority combine past and future elements, suggesting dreams operate as a simulation engine that constructs possible-world scenarios by recombining available experiential data.

This maps directly to Schacter & Addis's waking constructive simulation: the brain takes pieces of various past experiences and reassembles them into imagined scenarios. The 2022 study shows this same mechanism operates during sleep, without conscious goal-direction.

## Implication for the /dream Skill

Dreams are not purely retrospective or purely prospective — they are *constructive recombinations*. A well-designed dream pass should:
- Source inputs from multiple past experiences/chronicles (not just recent ones)
- Target scenarios that anticipate upcoming challenges or novel contexts
- Allow "fragment mixing" — scenarios that don't directly replay any single experience but combine elements in novel ways

This supports the CCE concept's architecture: reading SOUL.md + recent chronicle + world-model, then generating novel combinations, not replays.
