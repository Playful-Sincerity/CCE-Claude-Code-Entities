# Thomas Kuhn — The Structure of Scientific Revolutions (1962)

## Core Claim

Scientific knowledge is paradigm-relative. The paradigm — a framework of exemplars, practices, and commitments — cannot be fully retrieved as a set of explicit rules. It is lived, not stated.

## Paradigms and Tacit Structure

Scientific practice involves knowledge that "is tacitly embedded in shared examples" (exemplars). Scientists learn what counts as a good problem, a good solution, and a good explanation by working through canonical examples — not by learning rules.

Crucially, this tacit knowledge "is not subject to paraphrase in terms of rules and criteria without essential change." When you try to write down the rules implicit in scientific practice, you inevitably distort them. The knowledge exists in the practice, not in any formulation of the practice.

## Incommensurability and Translation Failure

When paradigms shift, translation fails. Scientists in different paradigms cannot fully understand each other because:

1. **Meaning changes holistically** — theoretical terms get their meaning from their relations within a theory. Changing one term changes all related terms. There is no invariant vocabulary to translate between paradigms.

2. **Taxonomic incompatibility** — different paradigms carve nature at different joints. The "no-overlap principle" means incompatible categorical systems cannot be merged. Revolutionary change is not accumulation — it's wholesale restructuring of the conceptual space.

3. **Perception changes** — scientists in different paradigms "see the world differently." The world itself looks different through different paradigm lenses. Anomalies that trigger crisis are not "just facts" — they are facts made visible by the paradigm that preceded the crisis.

## Exemplars as the Core of the Paradigm

Kuhn argues that the exemplar — the concrete problem-solution pair that becomes the model for a research community — is more fundamental than the paradigm's explicit rules. Scientists learn to recognize similarity by working through exemplars, and this similarity-recognition is not rule-following: "perception of similarity cannot be reduced to rules."

This means: the most important knowledge in any knowledge system is knowledge of *how to apply it*, and that procedural knowledge is fundamentally tacit, example-anchored, and not fully articulable.

## Challenge to Retrieval-Over-Weights

Kuhn's work is the strongest counter-argument frame in this survey:

**The objection:** If paradigmatic knowledge is tacit, example-embedded, and resistant to explicit articulation, then there are limits to what retrieval can do. You cannot retrieve a worldview; you can only inhabit one. The rules and chronicles in world-model files capture the explicit, propositional layer — but the deep orientation that makes those rules mean something is not stored in files. It is constituted through practice.

**Applied to value-drift:** An entity's values are not just the rule-files it retrieves. They are the tacit orientations that make those rules legible in a particular way. If those orientations drift, the rules can remain word-for-word identical while being enacted differently. Kuhn predicts this — normal science proceeds by applying paradigmatic templates, but which templates apply and how is tacit, not retrieval-based.

## The Recovery Possibility

Kuhn doesn't fully close the door. He distinguishes:

- **Paradigm shifts** (wholesale tacit reorientation, revolutionary) — cannot be engineered, only lived through
- **Normal science** (working within a paradigm, puzzle-solving) — follows recognizable patterns, is cumulative, CAN be partially encoded in retrieval systems

For an AI entity, the implication is that *within* a stable value orientation, retrieval-over-weights works well for normal operations. The risk is paradigm-shift-like drift at the tacit level — which is precisely value-drift, and which retrieval alone cannot prevent. The Mirror pattern, behavioral baselining, and human review are the structural answers to this residual Kuhnian threat.

## Vocabulary the Project Could Use

- **Paradigm-level drift** vs **parameter-level drift** — tacit orientation vs explicit rule violation
- **Exemplar knowledge** — the deep template structure that makes rules applicable (cannot be fully retrieved)
- **Incommensurability failure** — when an entity has drifted far enough that its rule-interpretations no longer translate to intended meanings
